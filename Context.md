# Context — Background to *The Pillars of the Earth*

Everything in this file is real-world history or real-world structure that the novel sits on top of. Read it once before Day 1; refer back when a Day's notes mention something here.

---

## 1. The political backdrop — *The Anarchy* (1135–1153)

The novel's middle two-thirds take place during a 19-year civil war in England known as **The Anarchy.** A chronicler of the time famously wrote that during these years, "Christ and his saints slept."

How it started:

- **1120 — The White Ship disaster.** King Henry I's only legitimate son, William, drowned when his ship sank in the English Channel near Normandy. Most of the heirs to the English throne were on that one ship. England suddenly had no clear male heir.
- Henry I named his daughter **Matilda** (also called *Empress Maud* — she'd been married to a Holy Roman Emperor) as his heir, and made his barons swear to support her.
- **1135 — Henry I dies.** His nephew **Stephen of Blois** breaks his oath, races to England, and grabs the throne for himself. Matilda invades from France to take what was promised to her. **Civil war.**

What it actually looked like on the ground:

- No clear front line. Barons picked sides based on which one was closer.
- Castles changed hands. Towns were burned. Crops were stolen.
- The king's law collapsed in many regions — local lords ruled by force.
- This is the world the Hamleighs thrive in: a brutal man can rise quickly when no central authority is strong enough to stop him.

How it ends:

- **1153 — Treaty of Wallingford.** Stephen agrees that Matilda's son **Henry** (of the House of Plantagenet) will inherit the throne when Stephen dies.
- **1154 — Henry II becomes king.** He's energetic, ruthless, and very effective. He spends the next decades rebuilding royal authority. The end of Part V and all of Part VI take place under Henry II.

## 2. The climax — *Becket's murder* (1170)

The novel's final act is built around a real event.

- **Thomas Becket** was Henry II's friend and chancellor. The king made him **Archbishop of Canterbury** (the most senior churchman in England) thinking Becket would be his loyal man inside the Church.
- Instead, once consecrated, Becket defended the Church's independence against the Crown — especially over whether priests could be tried in royal courts. He and Henry quarrelled bitterly for years.
- **December 29, 1170.** Four of Henry's knights — taking literally the king's frustrated outburst *"Will no one rid me of this turbulent priest?"* — rode to Canterbury and **murdered Becket inside his own cathedral**, during evening prayers.
- The shock across Europe was enormous. The pope canonised Becket within three years.
- **1174 — Henry II's public penance.** Henry walked barefoot to Canterbury and was flogged by the monks at Becket's tomb. The most powerful king in Europe, publicly humiliated by the Church.

In the novel, Philip and the Kingsbridge story line is woven into this real event. The final image of Henry's penance is what gives the book its sense of moral closure — the powerful brought low.

## 3. The architectural revolution — *Romanesque to Gothic*

The cathedral is a character in this book. The shift in *what kind* of cathedral matters.

**Romanesque** (~1000–1150): Thick walls. Small windows. Rounded arches. Dark interiors. Heavy. Squat. Massive piers. This is what every English church looked like when the novel starts. The first Kingsbridge cathedral, planned by Tom, is Romanesque.

**Gothic** (born ~1140): Three breakthroughs working together let you build *taller, lighter, brighter*:

1. **Pointed arches** — distribute weight downward more efficiently than rounded arches, so you can build higher.
2. **Ribbed vaults** — the ceiling weight runs along stone ribs to specific points, freeing the walls from bearing the load uniformly.
3. **Flying buttresses** — external supports that catch the outward thrust of the high walls, so the walls themselves can be thinner — and most importantly, full of *windows.*

Together these let you build cathedrals with walls of stained glass — flooded with coloured light. To a 12th-century person walking inside one, it would feel literally supernatural.

- **Saint-Denis abbey** near Paris, rebuilt under **Abbot Suger** in the 1140s, is generally credited as the first true Gothic building.
- **Canterbury, Wells, Salisbury** are the great early English Gothic cathedrals.
- In the novel, **Jack** travels to France (Paris/Saint-Denis) and Spain (Toledo) and brings this new architecture back to England. The cathedral he and Aliena finish at Kingsbridge is the first Gothic one in the country (within the fiction).

## 4. The secular hierarchy — who outranks whom

From the top down. This matters because half the conflicts in the book are about who has the right to tell whom what to do.

| Rank | What they are |
|---|---|
| **King** | Top of the heap. Owns all land in theory. Grants it to nobles in exchange for loyalty + military service. |
| **Earl** | (Latin: *comes*; French: *comte*.) The greatest nobles. Each Earl governs a whole county (a "shire") — hence the title. Aliena's father is **Earl of Shiring.** Major military and judicial power. |
| **Baron** | A noble who holds land directly from the king but isn't an earl. Lesser than an earl, greater than a knight. |
| **Knight** | A mounted warrior who holds a smaller piece of land (a "manor") in exchange for military service. **Sir** + first name. **William Hamleigh** starts as a knight's son. |
| **Freeman** | A commoner who is not bound to a lord — craftsmen like Tom Builder, merchants, prosperous farmers. Free to move, work, marry as they choose. |
| **Serf / villein** | A peasant tied to a particular manor. Can't leave without the lord's permission. Owes labour and a share of crops. Not a slave — but not free. The vast majority of the population. |

**Key idea: feudalism is land for loyalty.** A king gives an earl land. The earl gives some of that land to barons. Barons give some to knights. Knights extract labour from peasants who farm the land. Everyone owes military service "upward" and protection "downward."

When Aliena's father falls and the earldom is transferred, **everyone on those lands now owes loyalty to William Hamleigh instead.** That's why she has to win it *back* — the title is the legal mechanism that controls thousands of lives.

## 5. The Church hierarchy — parallel power structure

The Church in 12th-century England is almost a *second government*: it owns roughly a third of the land, runs its own courts, collects its own taxes (tithes), educates everyone literate, and answers to Rome — not to the king.

From the top down:

| Rank | What they are |
|---|---|
| **Pope** | Bishop of Rome. Head of all Western Christianity. |
| **Archbishop** | Senior bishop overseeing a large region. England has two: **Canterbury** (the senior one — primate of all England) and **York.** |
| **Bishop** | Oversees a **diocese** (a region with its own cathedral). Hugely powerful — both spiritually and politically. **Waleran Bigod** becomes Bishop of Kingsbridge. |
| **Abbot** | Head of an independent monastery (an **abbey**). |
| **Prior** | Head of a smaller monastery (a **priory**) — often a daughter house under an abbey. Sometimes (as at Kingsbridge) the priory is attached to a cathedral and runs it. **Philip** is Prior of Kingsbridge. |
| **Parish priest** | Looks after a single parish church and its congregation. The local face of the Church. |
| **Monk / nun** | Lives in a monastic community under vows. Not a priest (unless also ordained). Devotes life to prayer and work. |

**Key tension in the novel:** Prior Philip runs the monastery and the cathedral; Bishop Waleran is technically his superior in the Church hierarchy. But the cathedral and its lands belong to the *priory*, not to the bishop. So Waleran can give Philip orders but can't simply take his property. Most of their conflict is over this boundary — *who decides* about the cathedral, the market, the quarry, the wool fair.

**Why a "Prior" matters at a cathedral:** Kingsbridge cathedral is a **monastic cathedral** — run by a community of monks rather than by secular clergy. This was unusual but not rare in England (Canterbury, Durham, Worcester were all monastic). The head of the monks (the Prior) ran the cathedral day-to-day, even though the Bishop technically presided over it.

## 6. The medieval economy — *why wool matters so much*

The biggest plot engine in Aliena's storyline is the **wool trade.**

- 12th-century England's economy is dominated by **sheep.** English wool is the best in Europe.
- It's shipped to **Flanders** (modern Belgium) and northern Italy, where weavers turn it into cloth. That cloth is then sold back across Europe.
- Wool is bought once a year, after the spring shearing, at **wool fairs.**
- A **wool merchant** buys fleeces from many small farms, stores them, and sells in bulk to the big foreign buyers. Margins are large — if you have capital and you're smart, you can get *very* rich.
- This is why **Aliena rises**: starting from nothing, she walks farm to farm buying fleeces on credit, builds a network, and within a few years is the richest wool merchant in the county.

**A "fair" vs a "market":**
- A **market** is a weekly local thing — basic goods, run by a town under a royal charter.
- A **fair** is annual, regional, and huge — outside merchants, foreign buyers, a week of trading. Holding a fair was a privilege granted by the king, and incredibly profitable. **The fight between Kingsbridge and Shiring over the right to hold a fair is the economic spine of Parts III–V.**

## 7. Outlawry — what it meant to be Ellen

In medieval English law, an **outlaw** wasn't just a criminal at large. It was a legal status. To be declared outlaw was to be placed *outside the law's protection* — anyone could kill an outlaw on sight with no penalty. Outlaws were stripped of all property and rights.

People became outlaws for serious crimes (or being unjustly accused) and could flee to the great forests, which were vast and largely lawless. Some lived for years in the woods, hunting deer (which legally belonged to the king and was a capital offence to take), surviving on the edges.

**Ellen has been an outlaw for over a decade** when Tom meets her. She lives by hunting and by stealing from the road. Jack has never lived inside a building. This is why Jack first sees a cathedral and is dumbstruck — he has no frame of reference for any building larger than a hut.

## 8. Justice and the law

A few legal concepts that come up:

- **Trial by combat.** Two parties (or champions on their behalf) fight; God is presumed to give victory to the just. Used to settle serious disputes between nobles. Richard fights for the earldom this way later in the book.
- **Trial by ordeal.** Walking on hot iron, plunging a hand in boiling water — the wound's healing in three days was taken as God's verdict. Falling out of use in the 12th century; banned by the Church in 1215.
- **Hue and cry.** When a crime was witnessed, the witness was legally required to raise the alarm; every able-bodied man within hearing was obliged to join the chase.
- **Manorial courts.** The lord of a manor judged disputes among his serfs. He was, effectively, the police, the judge, and the executioner.
- **Royal justice.** The king's travelling judges (after Henry II's reforms) brought central law to the shires — one of the foundations of English **common law.**

## 9. The Church in daily life

- **Tithes.** Every household paid one-tenth of their produce to the Church.
- **Mass.** Conducted in Latin. Most ordinary people understood little of it but attended faithfully.
- **Sacraments.** Birth (baptism), marriage, death (last rites) — all in the hands of priests. The Church controlled the gates of every important life passage.
- **Saints and relics.** A church's prestige depended on what holy relics it owned — a saint's bones, a fragment of the True Cross. Pilgrims came to pray to relics, leaving money. **A cathedral with a famous saint was a money-making operation.** This is why Kingsbridge venerates **Saint Adolphus** — the relics bring pilgrims, the pilgrims bring offerings.
- **Marriage and annulment.** Marriage was a sacrament. Divorce did not exist. But a marriage could be **annulled** — declared never to have been valid — usually if it was unconsummated, or if the partners were too closely related. This is the plot mechanism that frees Aliena from Alfred.

## 10. Quick translation guide

- **Mason** — stone worker. **Master mason** = the architect/foreman of a building project. Tom's profession.
- **Jongleur** — wandering French musician/storyteller. Jack's father.
- **Cell** — a tiny outpost monastery, often only a handful of monks. Philip ran one before Kingsbridge.
- **Chapter** — both (a) the daily meeting where monks read a chapter of the Rule of St Benedict, and (b) the building where they meet ("chapter house").
- **Cloister** — the covered walkway around a square garden in a monastery.
- **Nave** — the long body of a church where the congregation stands.
- **Transept** — the cross-arms of a church (cathedrals are built in a cross shape).
- **Choir / chancel** — the part of the church near the altar, where the monks/clergy sit.
- **Quarry** — where stone is cut. Without a quarry, you cannot build a cathedral. Philip and Waleran fight over one for a hundred pages.
- **Fleece** — a single sheep's worth of wool, sold whole.
- **Sack** — the bulk unit of wool trade. A "sack of wool" = roughly 165 kg.
- **Mark** — a unit of money equal to two-thirds of a pound (13 shillings 4 pence). Used for large sums.
- **Shilling** — twelve pence. A skilled mason's daily wage was on the order of 2–4 pence in this era.

## 11. Worth remembering when narrating

- This is a world where **most people will die within a few miles of where they were born.** When Jack walks to Spain, your friend should grasp that it's the equivalent of going to the moon.
- This is a world without **clocks** (mechanical clocks were invented later in the 13th century — earlier, time was kept by sundials, candles, and the bells calling monks to prayer eight times a day).
- This is a world where **literacy is the Church's monopoly.** Outside of monks, priests, and a handful of clerks, almost nobody can read — including most nobles. Aliena reading and doing accounts is unusual; Jack drawing architectural plans is exceptional.
- This is a world where **a cathedral takes generations.** The men who broke the first ground rarely lived to see the consecration. That's the deepest theme of the book: building something larger than your own life.

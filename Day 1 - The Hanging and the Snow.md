# DAY 1 — "The Hanging and the Snow"

*Part I · ~20 min spoken*

**Tonight's question:** *How does a homeless mason end up building the most beautiful church in England?*

---

[slow, almost a whisper]

England. The year 1123. A small market town. Cold morning — the kind of cold where your breath shows.

A crowd has gathered around a wooden gallows. They've come to watch a hanging. The man on the platform is a stranger — a Frenchman, they say. A *jongleur.* A wandering minstrel, the kind who travels from village to village with a fiddle and a few songs. Nobody really knows what he did. The official charge is theft. A jewelled chalice, taken from a church.

He's a tall man. Red hair. Foreign-looking. His hands tied behind his back.

In the crowd, three men are watching him die.

[pause]

The first is a knight. Big, hard man. He looks bored.

The second is a young monk. He won't quite meet the prisoner's eyes.

The third is a priest. Black robes. Sharp, hungry face. He watches the hanging the way another man might watch a tradesman finish a job.

And at the back of the crowd — there's a girl.

[slow]

Young. Hooded. Red hair under the hood. Pregnant. Six, seven months along. Her eyes have not moved from the man on the platform.

[pause]

The hangman kicks the stool out.

The man on the rope kicks once. Twice. Then stills.

[pause]

And the girl — quietly, almost gently — looks at each of the three men in turn. The knight. The monk. The priest. And she says, loud enough that everyone hears:

[whisper, but cold]

*"I curse you. I curse you to a slow death from a hidden sickness. I curse you with sorrow that lasts your whole life. I curse the men you become."*

Then she turns, and she walks into the forest.

And nobody — not the knight, not the monk, not the priest, not the crowd — nobody ever sees her again.

[pause, full beat]

That is the first scene of the book. You don't find out who the dead man was, or who the girl was, or why those three men really put him there, for another eight hundred pages.

But every single thing that happens next — every death, every betrayal, every stone laid in a cathedral that hasn't even been built yet — flows from that morning.

[pause]

---

Okay. Twelve years later.

We meet a man. **Tom Builder.** That's not his name, it's his trade — *Tom the Builder.* He's a master mason. Big man, kind face, calloused hands. The kind of man who can look at a pile of stones and see a wall, look at a wall and see a building, look at a building and see God.

And he has a dream. One dream. Tom Builder has spent his entire working life dreaming of building a cathedral.

Not a house. Not a chapel. A *cathedral.* With towers. With arches. With a roof so high a man inside it feels small in a holy way. He's never done it. Most masons never get to. There aren't that many cathedrals being built.

Tom is currently building a house. A rich man's house. His wife **Agnes** is heavily pregnant, due any week now. His son **Alfred** — big, slow lad, takes after his father in size if not in heart. His little daughter **Martha** — small, watchful, kind.

And one morning, the man paying for the house rides up and says: *I've changed my mind. I don't want the house anymore.*

He pays Tom for the work done. Tom counts the coins. It's enough for maybe two weeks.

And then it's winter.

[pause]

You have to understand how dangerous unemployment was in 1135. Masons don't save — they live job to job. There's no charity that scales. The roads are dirt tracks between forests. In those forests there are wolves, and outlaws, and the kind of cold that kills.

Tom puts his tools on his back. Agnes carries the smallest child when she can't walk anymore. They walk from village to village. Tom asks at every monastery, every manor, every parish church — *do you need a mason?* And every answer is no.

By December, they're starving.

[slow]

Now I want you to picture this scene. Because this is the scene that breaks Tom Builder, and the whole book turns on it.

It's snowing. They're somewhere in the middle of a forest, miles from the nearest village. Agnes goes into labour. Right there. In the snow.

Tom builds her a shelter out of branches. He starts a fire. Alfred fetches water. Martha holds her mother's hand and cries quietly, because she's a child and she doesn't know what else to do.

The baby comes. A boy. Healthy. Crying.

But Agnes — Agnes won't stop bleeding.

[pause]

Tom watches his wife die in the snow. There's nothing he can do. He's a builder. He builds walls. He can't build a wall against this.

She dies holding the baby.

[pause, full beat]

And here's where Tom makes the choice that the rest of the book will judge him for. He has no milk. He has no money. He has two children to keep alive. He has miles of forest in every direction. And he has a newborn who needs feeding within hours or he'll die.

Tom buries Agnes under stones in the snow. He lays the baby on her grave, wrapped in his own cloak. And he walks away. With Alfred. With Martha. He tells himself the baby will die quickly, in the cold, and won't suffer.

He cannot bring himself to look back.

[whisper]

But somebody is watching.

[pause]

In the trees, twenty paces away, two pairs of eyes have seen this whole scene. A woman — wild-looking, dressed in leather and fur, hair tangled. And a boy beside her, eleven or twelve, with bright red hair.

The woman is called **Ellen.** The boy is called **Jack.**

Ellen lives in the forest. Outlawed years ago for reasons no one explains yet. She raises her son alone, hunts her own food, knows every track and every clearing for fifty miles.

She watches Tom walk away. She watches the baby on the grave.

And then, from the other direction down the path — a priest comes walking. With a goat on a lead. A wandering priest. He hears a baby crying. He stops. He picks the baby up. He looks around — sees no one — and walks on, with the baby tucked into his cloak.

Ellen and Jack watch the priest carry Tom's son away.

Tom never knows. Tom walks on. Tom believes his son is dead.

[pause]

That night, Tom can't sleep. He turns around. He goes back. He has to know. He has to see the baby's body. He has to bury him properly.

And when he gets back to Agnes's grave — the baby is gone.

Tom thinks: *wolves. Wolves took him.*

And he sinks to his knees in the snow next to his wife's grave, and finally — *finally* — Tom Builder weeps.

[pause]

That's when Ellen steps out of the trees.

[pause, almost a smile in the voice]

Tom doesn't know what she saw. He doesn't know about the priest. He doesn't know his son is alive. What he knows is that a strange wild woman has appeared in the snow, with a red-haired boy behind her, and she's looking at him not with pity but with — something else. *Recognition,* almost. Like she's been waiting for him.

She gives them food. Bread, dried meat — the kind of food the family hasn't seen in weeks. She lets them sleep by her fire.

In the morning, Tom asks her: *Do you know where a builder can find work?*

And Ellen says: *Walk west. Three days. There's a town called* ***Kingsbridge.*** *They have a cathedral. The prior is old. The cathedral is falling down. They'll need a builder soon.*

[pause]

---

Let's leave Tom on the road with two children, a wild woman, and a red-haired boy he doesn't yet love. And let's go meet the other man who matters.

[brisker, shift gears]

**Prior Philip.**

Philip is Welsh. Small dark man. Thirty-five. Devout. Genuinely, deeply, simply devout — he believes in God the way other men believe in their own hands.

He's the prior of a tiny monastic cell out in the forest — eight monks, a vegetable garden, a chapel. Quiet life. Philip runs it so well that they're actually self-sufficient, which most monasteries are not.

One evening his brother arrives. **Francis.** Francis is a clerk in the household of a powerful earl — close to the court, close to the king. And Francis has heard something he shouldn't have. He's heard that one of the great barons of England is plotting to overthrow King Stephen and put another claimant on the throne.

Civil war is coming. The kind that levels villages.

Francis can't go to the authorities himself — he'd be killed. He tells Philip. He says: *Tell someone you trust in the Church. Get word to the king.*

Philip thinks. Who does he trust? He thinks of a young, ambitious priest he's met once — a man named **Waleran Bigod.** Sharp face. Black robes. Hungry eyes. Philip rides off to find him.

[pause]

Now — hold that name. **Waleran Bigod.** Just hold it. You're going to hate him eventually.

[pause]

Philip finds him. Tells him about the plot. Waleran listens carefully. Thanks him. Says he will handle it.

Philip rides home feeling that he has done his duty before God and king.

He has no idea what he's just set in motion.

[pause]

Some weeks later, Philip is summoned to the cathedral town of **Kingsbridge.** The old prior has died. The monks need a new prior. They've heard about the Welsh monk who runs his little cell so well. They want him.

Philip arrives at Kingsbridge for the first time. He's elected prior. He's now in charge of a cathedral.

A cathedral which is, in his honest professional opinion, half-rotten. The roof is sagging. The walls have cracks you could put your fist into. He looks at it and thinks: *This building is going to fall down in ten years, and I have no money to fix it.*

[pause]

That same night — that *very night* — Tom Builder, with Agnes dead behind him and a wild woman beside him and two children and a red-haired stepson, walks into Kingsbridge for the first time. Late. Cold. Hungry.

He goes to the priory. He asks the new prior — Philip — for work. Any work.

Philip is sympathetic. He's seen Tom's tools. He can tell Tom is a master. But Philip has no money. He apologises. He gives Tom a meal. He says: *I'm sorry. I cannot help you.*

Tom thanks him. He leads his family back to the cathedral porch, where they curl up under their cloaks. Another night. Another no. Tomorrow they walk on.

Tom sleeps.

[pause]

At some point in the night — Tom wakes up.

The air smells wrong.

[slow]

There's a glow. An orange, flickering glow. He sits up. He looks at the cathedral above him.

The roof is on fire.

[pause]

By morning, half the cathedral is rubble. Smoking, blackened rubble. Nobody knows how it started. A candle, maybe. A spark. An act of God.

The monks stand in the ruins and weep.

Philip — new prior, three days into the job — stands in the ashes and realises that the building he was supposed to keep standing has just come down around him.

And next to him — quietly, calmly — stands a master mason.

Who has been dreaming of building a cathedral his entire life.

Who happens to be standing in the exact spot where one needs to be built.

Tom Builder looks at the ruined stones. His eyes fill with tears — but they're not sad tears. They're something else.

[whisper]

He starts to smile.

[pause, full beat]

---

[normal voice]

That's where we stop tonight.

Tomorrow: Tom gets the job he's been waiting his whole life for. A seventeen-year-old noblewoman called **Aliena** has everything taken from her in one night, and swears revenge in a way that will redraw the map of England. A villain you will *love to hate* steps onto the page. And the priest with the hungry eyes — **Waleran Bigod** — turns out to have used Philip's secret for purposes Philip cannot yet imagine.

Sleep on that.

---

## Delivery notes

- **The two whispered curse lines** are your "magic moments." Rehearse those out loud at least twice. Everything around them is just transport — those are the destination.
- **The Agnes death scene** — resist the urge to rush. Snowfall, branches, the fire, Martha's hand. Picture it as you talk; your friend will picture it too.
- **The Kingsbridge fire ending** — the whole 20 minutes is engineered to land on Tom's smile. Don't smile yourself when you say "he starts to smile" — say it flat. Let your friend's imagination do the work.
- **If you forget a line, don't recover by speeding up.** Slow down. Storytelling forgives a pause; it doesn't forgive a panicked rush.

## Cue legend

- `[pause]` — beat for breath, ~1 second
- `[pause, full beat]` — longer pause, ~2–3 seconds
- `[slow]` — drop the pace
- `[whisper]` — drop the volume
- `[brisker, shift gears]` — pick up tempo, new section

DAY 4 — “Love and Vengeance”

Part IV · ~20 min spoken

Last time: Tom Builder died in the smoke of a wool fair, killed by William Hamleigh on horseback. The cathedral he had given his life to was half-built and half-burning. His stepson Jack put the fires out and kept the work moving. And Aliena, in a room above her warehouse, told her brother Richard that she wanted William Hamleigh dead.

Tonight’s question: What does a woman who has built her life around saving her brother do when the only way to save him is to destroy herself?

[slow, opening]

Tom Builder is buried in the cemetery of Kingsbridge priory. Philip reads the service. Ellen, his common-law wife, stands at the graveside with her face set like she’s chewing nails. Martha, Tom’s daughter, eighteen, weeps quietly. Alfred, his son, stands with his hands clenched. And Jack — his stepson — does not weep. He stands at the back. He looks at the half-finished cathedral over the cemetery wall, and what he’s thinking is: this is mine now.

[pause]

But it isn’t his. Not yet.

[brisker]

The morning after the funeral, Philip calls a meeting of the senior masons in the chapter house. The question is simple. Who is master mason of Kingsbridge cathedral now?

There are two candidates. Alfred, Tom’s son, twenty-eight, senior journeyman, has been on the project from day one. He is competent. He is Tom’s blood.

And Jack, Tom’s stepson, twenty, a brilliant sculptor and — though nobody quite says it out loud — a more original mind than Alfred will ever be. But Jack is twenty. He is a stepson, not a son. And in the trade, seniority is everything.

Philip does what any prior of any priory would do in 1145. He gives the job to Alfred.

Jack is offered the position of deputy master mason. Carving, design support, the things Alfred cannot do. The implication being clear: Jack will do the brilliant parts, and Alfred will get the title.

Jack swallows it. Just barely. He swallows it because he loves the cathedral more than he hates his stepbrother. For now.

[pause]

[shift to Aliena]

Aliena, meanwhile, is doing the only thing she has ever done since she was seventeen. She is working. The wool business is huge now. She has a warehouse, agents, foreign contacts in Flanders, a reputation. And every penny that doesn’t go back into the business goes into a chest under her bed — a chest of silver — which she has been quietly filling for one purpose.

The purpose is Richard. Her brother. Sir Richard now, knighted, but landless. Aliena has been waiting for the right moment to buy Richard the men and arms he needs to march on Shiring and take back the earldom. The civil war is still grinding on across England — Stephen against Matilda — and a private army, in 1145, is something a person with money can simply acquire.

She needs about a hundred mercenaries. Mounted. Armed. Paid by the month.

The chest under the bed is not yet full enough.

[pause]

[slow, romantic gear]

Now — Jack and Aliena.

This has been simmering for three years and tonight we let it boil.

Aliena is twenty-eight now. Jack is twenty-one. The age gap matters in 1145 less than it matters now — but it matters. She has thought of him as a boy for a long time, partly because that was easier.

What changes it is a small thing. Jack carves a wooden box for Martha — his stepsister — as a gift. Beautiful, small, with a tiny image of Kingsbridge cathedral on the lid. Aliena sees the box. She asks him to make one for her. He does. He brings it to her warehouse one evening.

She opens it. Inside is a small carving — a wooden image of her face, in profile, smiling. The kind of carving you make for someone you love.

She looks up at him. And — for the first time, after three years of him watching her — she sees him.

[pause]

That night, in the small workshop behind her warehouse, with the rain coming down outside, Aliena and Jack come together.

They have one night.

[pause]

They have one night because Jack has to leave Kingsbridge before dawn to ride to the quarry; because Aliena cannot be seen with him publicly; because Alfred is in town and Alfred has been openly courting Aliena for months and Alfred is dangerous when he doesn’t get what he wants.

It is the only night they get.

And Aliena gets pregnant from it.

[pause]

She does not know it yet, that morning. But within a few weeks she will.

[pause]

[shift gears, harder]

Now — Alfred’s move.

Two weeks after Jack rides to the quarry, Alfred comes to Aliena’s house alone. He knocks. She lets him in.

He has clearly rehearsed this.

He says: Aliena. I want to marry you.

She says: No, Alfred. I don’t love you.

He says: Hear me out.

[slow]

And he lays out a deal.

He says: I know about your brother. I know what you want for him. I know what it costs. I have inherited my father’s money. I will pay for an army. A hundred mounted men. A year’s wages, in advance. Richard can ride on Shiring tomorrow if you say yes to me today.

[pause]

Aliena sits very still.

She has been buying fleeces for ten years to fill that chest under her bed. It is two-thirds full. Maybe three-quarters. Even three-quarters is not enough.

[pause]

She says: Why?

Alfred says: Because I want you. And because Jack will hate it.

[pause]

That last sentence is the only thing in the entire offer that’s honest.

[pause]

[slow]

I want you to sit with this scene for a second. Because everything in the book up to now has been pointing at it.

Aliena is twenty-eight. She has spent her entire adult life since the day William Hamleigh held her brother and forced him to watch — she has spent every day of every year since then trying to get her brother his earldom back. Trying to undo that scene. Trying to make Richard’s life worth the day she could not save him from.

And here, in front of her, is a man she does not love offering to make it happen tomorrow.

And in her belly — although she does not know it yet for certain — is the child of the man she does love.

[pause]

She says yes.

[pause, full beat]

[normal]

Jack comes back from the quarry three days later. He goes to her house. She is not there. He waits.

The next morning, on the steps of the church, he finds out. The whole town finds out. Aliena and Alfred are to be married. By the bishop. In a month.

Jack does not speak to Aliena. He does not go to her. He goes back to the cathedral, climbs up the scaffolding, and chisels stone for nine hours without a break. By the end of the day his hands are bleeding. He cannot feel them.

He does not eat. He does not sleep.

[pause]

The wedding happens. Aliena, in a green dress that costs more than most peasants will earn in a lifetime, stands next to Alfred at the altar of the half-finished cathedral. Waleran Bigod officiates personally — because of course he does, because Alfred is allied with Waleran’s faction, and this marriage is a public defeat for Philip and a public victory for Waleran. Aliena says the words. She does not look at Jack — who, despite everything, is in the crowd.

She moves into Alfred’s house.

[pause]

That night, Alfred takes her to bed. And cannot.

[pause]

Cannot. Cannot perform. Like William Hamleigh on his wife. Different reason — Alfred is not impotent, he is humiliated. He knows what every man in the town is whispering. He has bought a woman who does not want him. Every time he reaches for her, his body refuses.

This will go on for months. The marriage is never consummated. Never. Not once.

And Alfred — being who he is — blames Aliena. He starts hitting her. Within a year, he is beating her regularly. She is bruised. She wears long sleeves.

[pause]

She gives birth seven months after the wedding. A boy. Bright red hair.

Alfred does the math. Seven months. Red hair. He knows.

He doesn’t kill the baby. But he refuses to let Aliena nurse him. He gives the baby to a wet nurse in another house. Aliena is allowed to visit. She visits every day. She names the baby — quietly, only to herself, never to Alfred — Tommy. After Tom Builder, who was the closest thing to a father she had after her own father died.

[pause]

[shift to Jack]

Jack, meanwhile, has been deteriorating for months. He cannot work. He drinks. He fights. He is, by every external measure, falling apart.

One night, in early winter, Jack walks out of Kingsbridge.

He takes nothing with him except his tools — chisel, mallet, square — and a small purse of coins. He walks south. He does not say goodbye to Ellen. He does not say goodbye to Martha. He does not say goodbye to Philip.

Philip realises he has gone, the next morning, when Jack does not appear at the cathedral, and Jack’s bed has not been slept in, and there is a piece of parchment on Philip’s desk that says, in Jack’s careful lettering:

[whisper]

“I am going to find out who my father was. Pray for me.”

[pause]

[brisker]

He gets to Dover. He gets on a ship. He crosses the Channel — alone, in winter, with no plan beyond south.

He has heard rumours. He has heard that in France there are new buildings being raised that do not look like English cathedrals. Lighter. Taller. Walls full of glass. He has heard that in Spain there are scholars who know things Western Europe has forgotten — Arab mathematicians, Jewish philosophers, libraries that run for miles.

He has also heard one other thing. He has heard, from his mother Ellen, long ago, that his real father was a French jongleur named Jack who came to England with a Norman delegation and was hanged in a small town for stealing a chalice. Jack has never been told the name of the town. He has never been told the names of the men who put his father on the gallows. Ellen has refused to say. Ellen, for over twenty years, has kept that knowledge to herself like a wound she is afraid to open.

But she let him go. The morning after he disappeared, Ellen received a letter — slipped under her door — that said: I love you, Mother. I will come back. Wait for me.

She read it three times, folded it, put it in her shirt against her skin, and went out into the woods and did not come back for a week.

[pause]

[shift to Richard]

While Jack is on a road in Normandy in winter — Richard, in England, is mustering an army.

Alfred has paid. A hundred men, on horse, in armour, fed and waged for a year. Richard rides at the head. Aliena rides with him for the first week — partly to make sure he is properly outfitted, partly to escape Alfred for a few nights.

They make camp ten miles outside the borders of William’s earldom. Richard sends scouts. Richard plans.

The campaign, when it starts, is slow. It is a guerrilla campaign — small raids, lightning attacks, retreating into the woods. Richard takes back one village. Then another. Then a small castle. He’s careful. He doesn’t fight battles he cannot win. He bleeds William’s men a hundred at a time.

William responds in the only way William knows. He burns villages where he suspects Richard’s sympathisers are hiding. He hangs old men in their own farmyards. He produces every reason any peasant has ever needed to support Richard instead.

The campaign is going to take years. But it’s going to work.

[pause]

[slow, closing]

The cathedral. While all of this is happening, Alfred is master mason and the cathedral is rising, slowly, in his hands. He is a competent builder of plain walls. He cannot do what Jack did. The carvings stop being beautiful and start being adequate. The pilgrims notice. The offerings drop a little.

And then — about a year after Alfred takes over — Alfred makes a structural decision that he should not have made.

He decides to vault the choir in stone. The original plans had called for a timber ceiling. Alfred wants to be remembered. He wants a stone ceiling like the great French cathedrals. He hasn’t been to France. He hasn’t seen one. He thinks he can work it out from descriptions.

The walls of the choir were built to hold a timber roof. They are not built to hold the weight of stone.

A summer’s day, the masons are away at midday meal. There is a noise like a long groan from the choir. And then a cracking. And then — with the masons running back across the priory yard — the ceiling of the choir comes down.

Stone. Tons of it. Onto the floor of the church.

Nobody is killed, by sheer luck. But the choir is destroyed. A year of work is rubble. Alfred — who has been telling everyone for months that he is a great builder — is in front of the priory weeping with his hands in his hair.

Philip stands in the rubble. He says — to nobody in particular —

[whisper]

“We need Jack.”

[pause]

[normal]

That’s where we stop tonight.

Tomorrow: Jack arrives at a monastery outside Paris and walks into a building unlike any he has ever seen. He learns the truth about his father — the truth about who hanged him, and why. He comes back to England. The annulment of an unconsummated marriage is not, it turns out, very difficult to obtain — if you have the right witnesses, and the right bishop. And the curse from the prologue — twenty-five years in waiting now — begins, very quietly, to move.

Sleep on that.

Delivery notes

  • Alfred’s offer to Aliena — this is the cruelest scene in the book and it deserves the slowest delivery. Let her silence sit. Let “Why?” sit. Let “Because Jack will hate it” sit. Each one a beat.
  • The wedding night — flat, fast, no embroidery. “Cannot. Cannot perform.” That’s it. The horror is the matter-of-factness.
  • Jack’s note to Philip — whispered. “I am going to find out who my father was. Pray for me.” Lower volume than anything else in the session.
  • The choir collapse — let the sound do the work. “A noise like a long groan. And then a cracking. And then…” Pause before “the ceiling comes down.”
  • Closing line — “We need Jack.” Quiet. Don’t editorialise. Just let it sit.

# DAY 2 — "The Fall of the House of Shiring"

*Part II · ~20 min spoken*

**Last time:** *Tom Builder stood in the ashes of the burned Kingsbridge cathedral and smiled. A wandering Welsh monk, the new prior, looked at the ruins and at the master mason next to him and realised God had just delivered them both a problem disguised as a gift.*

**Tonight's question:** *How does the daughter of an earl, who has never worked a day in her life, become the richest wool merchant in England?*

---

[brisker, opening]

So Philip hires Tom. Of course he does. They have no money — Kingsbridge priory is broke, the bishop is hostile, half the cathedral is a heap of rubble — but they have a master mason who would build cathedrals for *food.* Philip and Tom shake hands.

And the work begins.

The first thing you do when you build a cathedral is *dig.* The old foundations have to be excavated, examined, often torn out. Trenches are cut deep into the earth until you hit bedrock. Stones are laid into the trenches that will never be seen by anyone — but the whole building, for the next four hundred years, rests on them. Tom is buried in this work. He's the happiest he's ever been in his life.

[pause]

But while Tom is digging foundations at Kingsbridge — half a county away, in the great hall of a castle, a different kind of foundation is being dynamited.

[slow, shift gears]

**Earl Bartholomew of Shiring.** The most powerful man in the region. Owns half the county. Sits in his own great hall, surrounded by his own knights, his own servants, his own family. And he is, quietly, organising a rebellion.

You remember from Day 1 — Philip told Waleran Bigod about a treason plot. He thought he was doing his duty. *That* was the plot. Earl Bartholomew. Trying to put Empress Matilda's son on the throne instead of King Stephen.

But Waleran Bigod did not take that information to the king. Not directly. Waleran took it to a small, ambitious family — landed knights, climbing — called the **Hamleighs.**

[brisker]

Three Hamleighs. The father — **Percy** — a knight, with a long, festering grudge against Earl Bartholomew for an insult years ago. The mother — **Regan** — sharp-faced, intelligent, the brains of the family. And the son — **William** — twenty, big, vicious, and a coward in the only way that matters.

Now William, a year before our story starts, had proposed marriage to Aliena, Earl Bartholomew's seventeen-year-old daughter. Aliena had laughed in his face. Not metaphorically — out loud, in front of guests, in front of his mother. William has not forgotten. His mother has *really* not forgotten.

So Waleran rides up to the Hamleighs and says: *I have news that will let you destroy Earl Bartholomew. I want a piece of it.* The Hamleighs say: name your price. Waleran says: a bishopric. He wants Kingsbridge.

[pause]

A deal is struck. The Hamleighs ride to the king with the treason news. The king is grateful — he sends men to take Bartholomew. The Hamleighs lead the attack themselves, at night. Bartholomew is dragged out of his castle in chains. The earldom is in legal limbo while the king decides what to do with it.

And the Hamleighs ride back to that castle the next morning. Not to take it yet — they haven't been granted the earldom yet. They've come to *gloat.*

[pause, slow]

---

I want you to picture this.

The great hall of Earl Bartholomew's castle. The servants have fled. The knights have been arrested or have run. There are two people left in the hall.

**Aliena.** Seventeen. Her father's heir in everything but title — he's raised her like a son, taught her to read, taught her household accounts. Beautiful, proud, used to being obeyed. Sitting in her own great hall with no idea what comes next.

**Richard.** Her younger brother. Fourteen. Big lad, going to be a big man. Brave in a thirteen-year-old way. Has never been tested.

The Hamleighs ride in. William at the head. He looks at Aliena. He smiles. It is not a warm smile. He turns to his men. He says:

[whisper, cold]

*Hold the boy.*

[pause]

Two men grab Richard. They make him watch.

And William rapes Aliena, in the great hall of her own home, in front of her brother.

[pause, full beat]

I am not going to dwell on this. Follett doesn't dwell on it either. It takes a paragraph. What matters is what it produces.

When William and his men leave — laughing — Aliena stands up. She finds her cloak. She finds a bag. She puts some food in it. She takes her brother's hand. And she swears, out loud, in the empty great hall:

[whisper, hard]

*"I will get our father out of that prison. I will get this earldom back for you, Richard. I will live for nothing else until it is done."*

[pause]

That vow — uttered at seventeen, in the worst hour of her life — is the engine of Aliena's character for the next twenty years.

[pause]

A word about Richard. Fourteen years old. Held by two men. Watched. *Did not fight.* Couldn't. Was held. But he saw. And Richard is going to spend the rest of his life trying to make up for that moment — and Aliena is going to spend the rest of her life making sure he gets to.

That's the deal between them. She earns. He fights. Until the earldom is back.

[pause]

---

So Aliena and Richard walk out of the castle gates with one shilling between them. Toward the nearest town, Winchester. Three days' walk. December.

The first month is bad. Aliena has been *raised* — she has not been *taught* anything that's useful for staying alive on a road in winter. She begs. She finds out begging is harder than working. She sleeps in stables. Richard catches a chill and nearly dies.

[brisker]

But Aliena is quick. She watches. She thinks.

In Winchester there's a wool merchant. She watches him for a week. Here's what he does: he buys fleeces from local farmers — one fleece at a time, retail — at a low price. He stores them. Then once a year, when the foreign buyers come up from Flanders for the great wool fair, he sells in bulk at a high price. The margin is enormous. The only thing you need to be a wool merchant is a little capital and a lot of legs.

Aliena has no capital. So she walks up to the merchant and offers him a deal. *Lend me one pound. I'll go to the villages you don't visit. I'll bring the fleeces back to you and you can sell them in your bulk. I'll take a small cut.*

He laughs. But she's persuasive — and beautiful — and there's something in her voice that he respects. He hands her a pound.

She walks. Village by village. Farms nobody else has bothered with. Old women selling a single fleece off a single sheep. She pays them fair. She comes back with a cartload. The merchant is amazed.

By the end of year one, Aliena is buying for him on credit alone. By the end of year two, she's her own master, with her own warehouse. By the end of year three, she's one of the great wool merchants of the county.

[pause]

Now — pause and feel this. Aliena. Seventeen, raped, dispossessed, walking from farm to farm in the mud — by twenty-one, *rich.* By her own hands. In a world that says women cannot do this. There are no other women in her trade. None. She is alone in it.

This is the great rebuilding story of the book. And we're only one-fifth of the way in.

[pause]

---

Meanwhile — back at Kingsbridge.

[brisker]

Tom Builder is no longer digging foundations. The foundations are *in.* Stone is going up. The cathedral has the beginnings of walls. A skeleton of a building you can actually see.

Tom is fifty now. Prosperous, finally. He has Ellen by his side — they've never officially married, the Church has objections, but they're a couple in every way that counts. He has Martha, his small daughter, growing up around stone dust. He has Alfred — his son, working as a journeyman mason now, big and competent and resentful. And he has **Jack.**

[slow]

Jack. Ellen's red-haired boy. Thirteen, fourteen, somewhere in there. Grew up wild in the forest, never inside a building. The first time he saw the cathedral being raised, he stared at it for an hour, silent.

Tom gave him a chisel and a piece of waste stone to play with one afternoon. Just to keep him busy.

By the end of that afternoon, Jack had carved a face. The face of a man. Not stylised — *recognisable.* The face of the journeyman next to him.

Tom looked at it. Looked at the boy. Looked back at the stone. And recognised — quietly, with no big speech — that he was looking at a kind of talent that does not appear once a generation. Maybe once a century.

Jack has no training. He just sees the shape that is inside the stone, and then he takes everything that isn't the shape away.

Tom doesn't say a word. He gives Jack better stone. He starts teaching him. And Alfred — Tom's actual son — watches this, and starts to hate his stepbrother. Quietly. The way only family can hate.

[pause]

---

[shift tone — political]

And now — **Waleran Bigod.**

Newly minted **Bishop of Kingsbridge.** The price the Hamleighs paid him for his treason news. He rides into the priory one afternoon to introduce himself to Philip.

Now remember — Philip and Waleran already know each other. Philip *trusted* Waleran. Told him about the plot. And Waleran turned that trust into a bishop's mitre and an earldom.

Their first conversation as bishop and prior is brief. Waleran says: *The new cathedral will be built at Shiring, not at Kingsbridge.*

Philip says: *No. The cathedral belongs to the priory. The priory is at Kingsbridge. It stays here.*

Waleran smiles his cold smile. He says: *We'll see.*

[pause]

And the war between them — which will last forty years — begins right there.

[pause]

[brisker]

Waleran's tactic is patient and relentless. He rides to the king and tells Stephen that Kingsbridge is a backwater, that a great new cathedral deserves a great new town, that Shiring — controlled by his Hamleigh allies — is the proper seat. Stephen, distracted by civil war, signs whatever paper is put in front of him. The bishop's chair is ordered moved to Shiring.

Philip rides to the king's court. Camps there for weeks. Calls in favours he barely has. By something he can only call the grace of God, he gets the order reversed.

Then Waleran tries again. Different angle. Tries to grant the quarry to Shiring instead of Kingsbridge. Tries to redirect the wool fair charter. Tries to move the high road.

Each time Philip fights. Each time, just barely, Philip wins.

And each time, the cathedral grows a few more feet.

[pause]

---

Where does the money come from? This is the slow miracle of Philip's tenure. Wool from the priory's sheep. Pilgrim offerings at the shrine of **Saint Adolphus** — Kingsbridge's relic, whose bones bring traffic. Quarrying done by the monks themselves to save labour costs. Timber from the priory's woods, sold off for ready cash. Philip becomes, of all things, a phenomenal businessman. He counts every penny. He plays a long game.

And Tom — Tom designs. He draws plans on parchment by candle. He shapes columns. He calculates loads in the way master masons did before there were such things as engineers — by feel, by tradition, by precedent. He has a small army under him now. Apprentices. Journeymen. And Jack, no longer a boy, carving capitals — the decorative stone tops of columns — that pilgrims come specifically to see.

[pause]

---

One more scene before we close.

[slow]

Aliena is twenty. She has money now. Real money. She has paid the king a fine to allow her brother Richard to be knighted — which by feudal law required a king's licence for the son of a disgraced earl. She has bought him a horse. Armour. A sword. He is a knight, Sir Richard. He has done what she swore.

But her father — Earl Bartholomew — is still in prison. And the earldom is still held by William Hamleigh, who's now had it confirmed by the king. Richard is a knight, but he holds no land. He has no men of his own. He cannot take on William.

Aliena rides to Winchester to visit her father in his cell. He's old. He's sick. He's only half-lucid. He looks up at her and asks:

*Have you given up?*

She kneels at his feet. She says: *No, Papa. I have not.*

He dies that night.

[pause]

And on the road back from his funeral, Aliena stops her cart. She looks down at a town in a valley below her. A river running through it. A market square. Good grazing for miles around. And a half-built cathedral at its heart, with scaffolding climbing up the sky.

She has been working out of Winchester for three years. It is time to move. She is going to build her business at a place where there is more wool flowing through and less competition.

The town she is looking at is **Kingsbridge.**

[pause, full beat]

---

[normal voice]

That's where we stop tonight.

Tomorrow: Aliena moves to Kingsbridge. A red-haired sculptor stops in the middle of his work the first time she walks past him. Master mason Tom Builder dreams of finishing the building he started — but he will not get to. And William Hamleigh, Earl of Shiring now, fully grown into his monstrousness, makes the worst day of the cathedral's life happen on purpose.

Sleep on that.

---

## Delivery notes

- **The hall scene** — the rape — handle short, handle plain, *do not embroider.* The horror is in what it does to Aliena, not in the act. One line: "William rapes Aliena, in the great hall of her own home, in front of her brother." Then move.
- **Aliena's vow** — this is the second of your "magic moment" whispered lines (the curse in the prologue was the first). It needs to land with the same weight. Slow down. Drop volume. Don't rush onto the next sentence.
- **The wool merchant beat** — pick up the tempo. This is montage. Years passing. Aliena rising. Don't slow until you reach "she is one of the great wool merchants of the county."
- **Closing image** — Aliena above the valley, looking down at Kingsbridge — speak it like an arrival. The cathedral, the river, the market. That's tomorrow.

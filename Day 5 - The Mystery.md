# DAY 5 — "The Mystery"

*Part V · ~20 min spoken*

**Last time:** *Aliena married Alfred to buy her brother an army. Alfred could not consummate the marriage and began beating her. She gave birth to Jack's son in secret. Jack — heartbroken — walked out of England with his tools and a question. Alfred's stone ceiling collapsed onto the cathedral floor. And Prior Philip, standing in the rubble, said quietly: "We need Jack."*

**Tonight's question:** *Who really hanged the man in the prologue, and why?*

---

[brisker, opening]

We follow Jack now. Across the Channel in winter. Through Normandy. He walks. He sleeps in barns and monasteries. He earns small coin where he can carving small things at small churches. He moves south, then east. He has heard there is something he needs to see, just outside Paris.

He arrives at the **abbey of Saint-Denis** in 1147 or thereabouts.

[slow]

Now you need to know what this place is. Saint-Denis is an old royal abbey, a few miles north of Paris. The Kings of France are buried there. The current abbot is a man named **Suger** — an extraordinary administrator and an even more extraordinary visionary. Suger has just finished rebuilding the choir and the west front of the abbey church.

And it does not look like any building in Europe.

[pause]

Jack walks through the front doors. He looks up. And he sees something he cannot at first understand.

The walls of the choir are not really walls. They are *frames.* Frames around enormous panels of coloured glass. Stained glass — reds, blues, golds — covering most of the upper part of the church. The light comes through them and falls onto the stone floor in shifting pools of colour.

The arches are not rounded. They are pointed. Where two of them cross, the ceiling is held up by a *rib* — a stone spine that takes the weight to a single point. Which means the wall underneath doesn't have to do the work. Which means the wall can be *thin* — and full of *glass.*

[pause]

Jack stands in the choir of Saint-Denis and starts to cry.

Because he sees, all at once, that everything he has been building in Kingsbridge is the old way. Romanesque. Thick walls. Tiny windows. Heavy. Dark. The cathedral he has been working on his entire adult life is — by comparison to what is in front of him — a *cave.*

What is in front of him is *light made into a building.*

He walks out of the church. He sits on a low wall in the courtyard. He sits there for two hours, not moving. And then he gets up and goes to find the master mason of Saint-Denis and asks if he can stay and work.

[pause]

---

[brisker]

Jack stays at Saint-Denis for about a year. He works. He learns. Pointed arch — invented to redistribute load. Rib vault — for the same reason, and to free the walls. Flying buttress — the external scaffolding of stone that catches the outward thrust of high walls, so the walls themselves can be made of glass.

These three innovations, taken together, are the start of what we now call **Gothic architecture.** Almost nobody in England has seen them yet. Jack is one of the first English masons to learn how they actually work.

He copies plans into a small leather-bound book. He sketches details. He talks to other masons who have worked at Chartres, at Sens, at Noyon. He has — without knowing it — apprenticed himself to the future.

[pause]

But he has not forgotten the other question. The question that drove him out of England in the first place.

*Who was my father?*

He asks. Carefully. *A man named Jack. A jongleur. Came to England with a delegation in 1123. Hanged. Did anyone here know him?*

For months, nothing. Then one night, in a tavern in Paris, an old man — really old, eighty maybe, blind in one eye — hears Jack ask the question and says: *I knew him.*

[pause, slow]

The old man's name is not important. What matters is what he tells Jack.

He says: *Your father's name was Jacques de Cherbourg. He was a jongleur. A minstrel. A storyteller. He went to England in the household of an embassy from the King of France. There was an incident at the court of the English king. A jewelled chalice — a gift from the French king to the English king — went missing the night the embassy arrived. The English wanted someone to blame. The French wanted to leave with their honour intact. Three men of the English court — a knight, a young monk, a priest — produced the chalice and produced Jacques and said he had stolen it. He was hanged the next morning in a small market town on the way back to the coast. The embassy was let go. Everyone said the matter was settled.*

The old man looks at Jack across the tavern table and says:

[whisper]

*Your father did not steal that chalice. The three men who hanged him stole it.*

[pause]

---

[slow]

Jack rides south. He goes to Spain. Specifically to **Toledo** — at this point, one of the most extraordinary cities in Europe. Christian, Muslim, and Jewish scholars working together on translation. Arab mathematics flowing into Latin. Greek philosophy, preserved by Arab scholars during the Dark Ages, being translated into the languages Western Europe can read.

Jack is at Toledo for two years. He learns geometry that almost no English mason knows. He learns to use the astrolabe. He learns proportional drawing. He becomes, slowly, the most technically educated master mason in England — although nobody in England knows it yet, because he isn't in England.

And he keeps asking about the chalice. About the embassy of 1123.

A Spanish bishop he is working for — building a small church — connects him to a French clerk who connects him to an old Norman knight who, twenty-five years later, remembers the affair.

The knight — drunk, in a tavern, at last — gives him the three names.

[pause]

The knight who produced the chalice was **Percy Hamleigh.**

The young monk who corroborated was a man named James — who became, in time, **Prior James of Kingsbridge.** The old prior whom Philip succeeded.

The priest who actually planned it, who took the chalice in the first place, was **Waleran Bigod.**

[pause]

[slow]

Now Jack sits with this. Slowly.

Percy Hamleigh. William's father. Long dead now. Untouchable.

Prior James of Kingsbridge. Dead before Philip arrived. Untouchable.

But Waleran Bigod is alive. Waleran Bigod is the **Bishop of Kingsbridge.** Waleran Bigod has been at war with Philip for twenty years. Waleran Bigod is the priest Aliena's son might one day kneel before to receive confirmation.

And Waleran Bigod was one of the three men whom — twenty-five years ago — a sixteen-year-old pregnant girl on the edge of a market crowd cursed in a voice loud enough for the whole town to hear.

[pause, full beat]

---

[brisker]

Jack starts walking back to England. It is, by now, 1150.

He has been gone four years.

He arrives in Kingsbridge on an afternoon in early summer.

[slow]

The town has changed. It is bigger. The cathedral is — strangely — almost the same as he left it. Alfred's choir collapse stalled the work badly. After that disaster the priory ran out of money and the project has been crawling. The roof of the choir is timber, hurriedly thrown up to keep the rain off. The nave is unfinished. The west front is half-built.

Jack walks through the town. Nobody recognises him at first — he is bearded, thinner, sun-darkened from Spain. He goes to his mother Ellen's cottage on the edge of the forest. He knocks. She opens the door.

She does not speak. She holds him for a long time. Then she says: *Aliena left Alfred two years ago. She lives in the priory guest house. She has been waiting for you.*

[pause]

He doesn't go to her that day. He goes to Philip first.

[pause]

---

[brisker]

He tells Philip the whole story. The chalice. The three men. Waleran.

Philip — who has been at war with Waleran for two decades and never quite known *why* Waleran was so willing to lie, so willing to cheat, so willing to burn villages to win — Philip listens with his hands flat on his desk.

When Jack finishes, Philip says: *I believe you. But the law does not work on hearsay from a tavern in Toledo. We need proof. The chalice itself, ideally. Or witnesses who can testify in a Church court.*

Jack says: *I will find them.*

Philip says: *Yes. Take your time. He's not going anywhere.*

Then Philip says: *Now. Tell me what you learned in France.*

[pause]

And Jack — who has not yet been to see Aliena, who has not yet met his son, who has been back in Kingsbridge for less than a day — Jack sits with Philip across that desk and starts drawing pointed arches on parchment.

Philip looks at the drawings. Looks at Jack. And Philip — the slowest, most cautious, most administrative man in the building — Philip says:

[whisper]

*We will build the most beautiful cathedral in England.*

[pause]

---

[slow]

Now — Jack and Aliena.

It is the next morning. Aliena is in the priory guest house. She has known for an hour, by gossip running through the town, that Jack is back. She is sitting on her bed, in her best dress, with her hands shaking.

He knocks. She opens. He stands in the doorway.

Behind her, in the room, a five-year-old red-haired boy looks up.

Jack looks at him. He looks at Aliena. He says, quietly:

*Tommy?*

Aliena nods.

[pause]

Jack does not hug her. Not yet. He goes to the boy first. He kneels. He says: *My name is Jack. I have been away a long time. I would very much like to be your friend.*

Tommy looks at his mother, who is now silently weeping. Tommy looks back at Jack. Tommy holds out a small wooden carving — a horse, that Jack had carved for him before he left, when Tommy was a baby and Aliena had hidden it from Alfred.

[pause]

That's the moment. Not the kiss. Not the embrace. The boy holding out the carving.

[pause]

---

[brisker, picking up]

The annulment is not difficult. Aliena testifies that her marriage to Alfred was never consummated. Two physicians examine her and confirm — by tests we will not go into — that she has had a child but has not had a husband, biologically speaking. Alfred himself, when called to testify, admits it, because he is by now broken and ashamed and would rather be free of her than fight for her.

Waleran tries to block the annulment on procedural grounds. He fails. Even bishops cannot block annulments forever when the evidence is this clear.

Aliena and Jack are married — quietly, by Philip, in the small priory chapel, on a Tuesday morning in the autumn of 1150.

Tommy is at the front. Ellen is at the front. Martha — Tom's daughter, still unmarried, devoted to Jack since they were children — is at the front.

And then they get back to work.

[pause]

---

[slow, building]

Jack becomes master mason. He redesigns the cathedral.

The nave will have pointed arches. The triforium will be glazed. There will be flying buttresses outside, taking the weight of the high vaults. The clerestory will be enormous — long bands of stained glass, twenty feet tall.

He starts by demolishing Alfred's choir entirely. The temporary timber roof comes off. The walls — built for Romanesque weight — are reinforced. New piers are dropped to support the rib vaults Jack is planning. Stone starts to come from the quarry again, in volume.

And — slowly, slowly — over the next several years — a different building begins to rise out of the foundations Tom Builder dug.

A *Gothic* building.

The first of its kind in England.

The light through the new clerestory, when the first windows are glazed, falls onto the floor of the nave in pools of red and blue and gold. The townspeople of Kingsbridge stand at the back of the church on the first Sunday it is opened to them and cannot quite work out what they are seeing.

Jack stands at the back of the church too, with Aliena's hand in his, and Tommy in front of them, and what he is thinking is —

[whisper]

*Two of the three are dead. One is left.*

[pause, full beat]

---

[normal voice]

That's where we stop tonight.

Tomorrow: Henry, the son Empress Matilda swore her barons to support, finally takes the throne as King Henry II. The civil war ends. The king picks his best friend Thomas Becket to be Archbishop of Canterbury and lives to regret it. William Hamleigh, getting old, finally answers for the wool fair and for forty years of cruelty. And the priest with the hungry eyes from the very first scene of the book — twenty-eight years after a sixteen-year-old girl cursed him in a market square — finds out what a curse, properly aged, can do to a man.

Sleep on that.

---

## Delivery notes

- **Saint-Denis arrival** — the moment when Jack walks in and sees Gothic for the first time is one of the great moments in the book. Slow this all the way down. He doesn't speak. He just looks. Then he sits on the wall. Then he cries.
- **The old man in the tavern** — drop volume to a near whisper for "Your father did not steal that chalice." This is the central revelation of the book and it's delivered by an unknown old man over wine. Honor that quietness.
- **The three names** — pause between each. Percy. James. Waleran. Each one a beat.
- **Tommy with the wooden horse** — don't oversell it. Children meeting their fathers are one of those scenes that destroys itself if you tilt sentimental. Flat delivery, let the image do the work.
- **The closing whisper** — "Two of the three are dead. One is left." This is your hand-off to Day 6. Like a fuse being lit.

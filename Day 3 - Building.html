DAY 3 — “Building”

Part III · ~20 min spoken

Last time: Aliena, twenty years old, having lost everything at seventeen, has clawed her way to wealth as a wool merchant. Her father is dead. Her brother is a knight. And she has just stopped her cart at the top of a valley and looked down at the town of Kingsbridge, where a half-built cathedral is climbing into the sky.

Tonight’s question: What kind of man builds a cathedral, and what kind of man burns one down?

[brisker, opening]

Aliena rides into Kingsbridge.

She goes to the priory. She asks to see the prior. Philip — older now, in his forties, grey starting at his temples — receives her in the chapter house. She tells him: I want to set up my wool business here. I need to rent a stone warehouse. I need to live in the town. And I need the priory’s protection.

Philip is wary at first. A woman, unmarried, wanting to set up a business — this is not what 12th-century English towns are organised around. But Philip has spent twenty years in administration and he can read a balance sheet. He knows what a wool merchant of Aliena’s reputation means for his town. More traffic. More tolls. More tenants. More taxes.

He says yes.

She rents from him. She builds a warehouse. She moves Richard’s small household into a house near hers. And she gets to work.

[pause]

And here’s the thing that nobody quite saw coming. Within a year, the town starts to grow around Aliena. Sellers come to her instead of to the merchants of Winchester. Farmers from a thirty-mile radius find that they can get better prices in Kingsbridge than they can anywhere else. Buyers from Flanders, hearing about her, route their ships and pack-trains through her warehouse. The annual Kingsbridge wool fair, which used to be a sleepy thing, starts to swell.

And as the trade flows in, money flows to the priory. Tolls. Rents. Pilgrim offerings. Suddenly Philip has a budget for the cathedral that he never had before.

The cathedral starts to rise faster.

[pause]

[shift gears, slow]

Now I want to talk about Jack.

Jack is seventeen when Aliena moves to Kingsbridge. He’s been carving stone for three years. He has surpassed every journeyman on the project. He has surpassed his stepfather Tom in fine work — not in design, not in load-bearing engineering, but in carving. He can sculpt the faces of saints into stone in a way that makes them look like they want to speak.

Jack is also strange. He grew up in the forest. He has no friends his own age — he didn’t grow up around any. He reads, which most people his age cannot. He has opinions about God that are mildly heretical. And he is, quite suddenly, in love with Aliena.

[slow]

She doesn’t know.

She walks past the cathedral scaffolding one day, on the way to her warehouse, and Jack — eighteen years old now — stops his chisel mid-stroke and just watches her go by. The other masons see this. They laugh at him. Jack doesn’t care.

He starts carving women’s faces into the capitals of columns. Just for himself. The faces all look like her. Everybody in Kingsbridge can see it. Everybody knows. Aliena is the only person in the town who does not realise that a brilliant young sculptor is carving her face into the walls of a cathedral.

[pause]

She’s twenty-six now. He’s eighteen. To her, he is a boy. A talented boy, yes — she’s not blind, she’s seen his work. But a boy. She is a wealthy widow-in-spirit-if-not-in-law. She isn’t thinking about love. She isn’t thinking about any man, because the only man she has ever known intimately was William Hamleigh in her father’s hall, and the memory of that has built a wall in her that no man can yet reach.

So Jack watches. And carves. And waits.

[pause]

[brisker]

Now — Alfred.

Tom’s actual son. Twenty-seven, twenty-eight by now. Senior journeyman on the project. Heir-apparent to the master mason title when Tom retires. Competent. Hard worker. Big strong hands. Limited imagination.

And Alfred — for years now — has been watching his red-haired stepbrother do work he himself cannot do. Watching Tom praise Jack. Watching the apprentices learn from Jack instead of from him. Watching Jack carve capitals that pilgrims come specifically to see, while Alfred lays plain ashlar walls.

Alfred hates Jack. Not loudly. Not all the time. Just in the way that a steady drip of resentment hates the thing it cannot match.

One afternoon, in the lodge — the workshop where the masons eat their midday meal — Alfred takes a swing at Jack. Just like that. No warning. They go down in the sawdust. Tom has to pull them apart. There’s blood. There’s a broken nose. There are no apologies.

That night, Tom sits Alfred down and tries to talk to him man-to-man. It doesn’t take. Alfred is not a man who can be talked to.

[pause]

I want you to hold all three of these threads. Jack, twenty, in love with Aliena and dreaming of stone. Aliena, twenty-six, wealthy, untouched, hauling fleeces. Alfred, twenty-eight, resentful, dangerous, watching the woman he wants prefer his brother.

Those three are going to crash into each other in a way that will break all of them.

[pause]

[slow, gear shift to William]

Meanwhile — William Hamleigh. Earl of Shiring. About thirty now.

Picture this man. He has had everything his mother promised him. The earldom. The land. The castles. The bannermen. He is — by every external measure — successful. His mother has even arranged a marriage for him.

And he is a wreck.

Two things have gone wrong for William inside himself. The first: he is terrified of hell. Genuinely. He cannot sleep some nights. He confesses constantly. He pays priests to pray for his soul. He believes — correctly — that he is going to burn.

The second: he is impotent with his wife. They have been married four years. There are no children. There will be no children. He does not say it out loud, but he knows it, and his mother knows it, and every servant in the household knows it.

And the rage that those two things produce in William — the rage that he is a damned man with no heir — that rage turns outward. Onto his serfs. Onto the villages he rides through. Onto every woman his men can drag in front of him to prove he is still a man. He is, by Year Six of his earldom, the most feared man in the county, in the way only a small frightened cruelty can be feared.

[pause]

And his hatred for Aliena — who refused him, who survived him, who is now rich in the town next to his — that hatred is the cleanest emotion in his life.

[pause]

So William makes his big move.

[brisker]

He rides to the king. He pays a substantial fee. He requests a royal charter for a wool fair at Shiring. Annual. Held the same week as the wool fair at Kingsbridge.

The king grants it.

This is direct economic warfare. The foreign buyers can only attend one fair. Half of them will go to Shiring. The Kingsbridge fair will be cut in half. The cathedral’s income will collapse.

Philip rides to court to fight it. He gets some concessions — different week, maybe — but the Shiring fair stands.

The Kingsbridge wool fair happens anyway. It’s smaller than last year. But it happens. Stalls go up. Bunting flies. Foreign buyers haggle over fleeces. Aliena is at the centre of it, in her booth, doing the biggest single day of trade of her career.

[pause]

And in the afternoon, William rides into the town with thirty mounted men.

[pause, slow]

I want you to picture this.

A market square. Hundreds of people. Wooden stalls. Bunting. Sheep. Stacks of fleeces stretched out under canvas awnings. Children running between the legs of merchants. The smell of roasting meat from food stalls. The shout of buyers and sellers and the bleating of animals.

And thirty men on horseback, in helmets, with swords drawn, come riding through the gates.

The first thing they do is set fire to the awnings.

[pause]

Canvas burns fast. The fire jumps from stall to stall. The fleeces — fleeces are oily, fleeces are flammable — go up like brushwood. Within minutes there is a wall of fire across the centre of the fair.

Now the crowd panics. Hundreds of people, packed into a small square, with fire to their north and horsemen with swords to their south. They stampede toward the only opening — the cathedral porch, the half-built west front of the cathedral itself.

Children fall and are trampled. Old men trip on stones. A woman is crushed against the priory wall by the press of bodies. William’s men ride through the crowd cutting at anyone who tries to organise.

[pause]

In the middle of this, Tom Builder is standing in the cathedral porch. He’s an old man now — sixty, maybe — but he’s the master mason and the cathedral is his. He’s trying to push people through the porch in an organised way, to keep more children from being trampled, when he sees William’s men starting to set fire to the scaffolding of the cathedral itself.

If the scaffolding burns, the cathedral burns.

[slow]

Tom Builder leaves the porch. He walks out into the burning square. He goes after William.

He gets to William’s horse. He grabs the bridle. He shouts something — we don’t know what, we never know what.

And William — already swinging — brings his sword down on Tom’s head.

[pause, full beat]

Tom Builder. Master mason. Has been dreaming his whole life of building a cathedral. Has been one quarter of the way through building one. Dies in the smoke of a wool fair, in front of the half-finished west front, killed by a small frightened man on a horse.

[pause]

When William rides out, the square is on fire and bodies are everywhere. Philip and Jack and Aliena run out of the smoke trying to find people. The cathedral’s scaffolding has caught in two places — Jack and the apprentices, soaking themselves in water from the priory well, climb up to put it out, while it’s still burning, and they save it.

But Tom is dead.

[pause]

Philip kneels in the ashes and prays. Ellen — Tom’s wife, although they were never married before the Church — kneels next to him and weeps. Martha — Tom’s daughter, eighteen now — kneels next to her. Alfred — Tom’s son — stands frozen.

And Jack — Tom’s stepson, the boy Tom picked up out of the forest and made into a mason — Jack stands next to Tom’s body, and then turns, and looks up at the half-built cathedral, and starts directing the apprentices on what to do next.

The work, in Jack’s hands, does not stop.

[pause]

That same evening, in a room above her warehouse, Aliena sits with her brother Richard. She has been at the fair all day. She has seen things she will not tell anyone about. She looks at Richard and she says:

“I want William Hamleigh dead. By any means. I will pay for it.”

Richard nods.

[pause]

[normal voice]

That’s where we stop tonight.

Tomorrow: Tom is buried. Someone has to be master mason of the cathedral now — and the two candidates are Tom’s son, and Tom’s stepson, and they hate each other. Jack and Aliena finally see each other, and what they see is dangerous. A young woman who has built her whole life around the rescue of her brother makes the worst trade of her life — and a young man who has spent his life waiting for one woman walks out of England with nothing but his tools.

Sleep on that.

Delivery notes

  • The Jack-watching-Aliena-walk-by beat — slow this one. It’s the most romantic moment in the book, and it’s silent. He stops, he watches, she doesn’t see. Don’t oversell it.
  • The wool fair fire — this is your second great setpiece (after Agnes in the snow). Build it slowly: market square, awnings, fleeces, children. The horsemen arrive almost as an interruption. The fire is fast; the panic is fast; but the setup should breathe.
  • Tom’s death — flat delivery. Do not editorialise. “He gets to William’s horse. He grabs the bridle. He shouts something — we don’t know what. And William brings his sword down on Tom’s head.” That’s the rhythm. No flourishes.
  • Closing image — Aliena, that night, telling Richard she wants William dead. Drop volume on her line. She’s been quiet for so long. Now she’s quietly declaring war.

DAY 6 — “Reckoning”

Part VI · ~20 min spoken

Last time: Jack came home to England with the names of three men. The man he murdered with that knowledge first was, in a sense, Alfred — whose marriage to Aliena was annulled. Jack and Aliena married. Jack became master mason and started raising a Gothic cathedral over Tom Builder’s old foundations. Two of the three men who hanged Jack’s father were already dead. One was left. Twenty-eight years after a girl had cursed him in a market square, his bill was about to come due.

Tonight’s question: How do you make a curse, sworn by a sixteen-year-old in a market square, actually come true?

[brisker, opening]

The civil war ends.

In 1153, Stephen and Matilda’s son Henry sign the Treaty of Wallingford. Stephen will reign for the rest of his short life; when he dies, Henry will inherit. The war is over. The chronicler who once wrote “Christ and his saints slept” puts his pen down.

In 1154, Stephen dies. Henry II — twenty-one years old, energetic, brilliant, ruthless — is crowned King of England. He immediately starts pulling the country back together. He builds royal courts. He sends judges around the country to enforce the king’s justice instead of the lord’s. The foundations of what becomes English common law are laid in his reign. Stray castles built by warlords during the Anarchy are pulled down. The country, slowly, becomes a country again.

[pause]

For Aliena, this matters. Henry’s accession means the political protection William Hamleigh has enjoyed under Stephen — Stephen who never paid attention, Stephen who signed any paper — that protection is gone. William is now just a brutal earl in a country with a king who cares about brutal earls.

For Philip, this matters too. Henry’s court takes a much harder look at corrupt churchmen than Stephen’s ever did. The bishops who climbed under Stephen — Waleran chief among them — find themselves with new enemies in high places.

The wind is changing.

[pause]

[brisker]

Richard’s campaign. Year four of the guerrilla war in Shiring.

Richard has been chipping away at William for years. Village by village. Castle by castle. Skirmish by skirmish. William has burned every village he suspected of harbouring Richard’s men — which has produced, exactly as you would expect, a thousand new sympathisers for Richard.

In 1155 or so — under Henry II’s new harder gaze — Richard finally fights a pitched battle at the gates of Shiring castle. William’s remaining men are demoralised, underfed, unpaid. They break. Richard takes the castle. William escapes on horseback with a handful of retainers and rides north, to a smaller, meaner estate that’s still legally his.

Henry’s court, asked to adjudicate, restores the earldom of Shiring to Sir Richard. The original injustice — the framing of Earl Bartholomew, all those years ago — is formally undone.

Aliena, now thirty-five, stands at her brother’s investiture in the great hall of Shiring castle. The same hall in which, eighteen years before, William had held Richard and forced him to watch. She does not weep. She does not embrace anyone. She just stands there, watching her brother kneel to the king’s representative and rise as Earl.

The vow that had run her entire adult life — I will get this earldom back for you, Richard — is, after eighteen years, complete.

[pause]

[slow, gear shift]

I have to talk about Alfred for a moment, because Alfred has one last scene in the book and it’s a hard one.

After the annulment, Alfred fell apart. He could not find another master mason job — his reputation had not survived the collapsed choir. He went to London. He drank. He gambled away the money his father had left him. He came back to Kingsbridge several years later, broken, asking Jack — Jack of all people — for work.

Jack, in one of the most generous gestures in the book, gave him a job. Senior mason. Plain wall work. Decent wages. A bed in the lodge.

It does not go well.

One night — months into this — Alfred breaks into the house Jack and Aliena share. He goes to Aliena’s bedroom. He has a knife. He drags her from the bed and tries to rape her — partly out of long-festering hatred, partly out of drunken hopelessness.

Aliena fights. She does not scream — she has learned, since seventeen, that screams do not save you. She fights silently with her elbows and her teeth.

Jack comes in. Then Richard, who is visiting. Then the priory porter. They pull Alfred off. They do not kill him — Jack stops them. They throw him out into the road in only his shirt.

Alfred is found three days later, in a ditch outside the town, dead. Whether from exposure, drink, or his own hand, nobody is certain. He is buried in a grave outside the priory wall — the only mark of dishonour Philip permits himself.

That is Alfred’s whole life. Built on resentment, finished in a ditch.

[pause]

[shift, brisker]

Now — the real history. Because the final act of this book is woven into one of the most famous events in medieval Europe.

In 1162, King Henry II appoints his best friend — his chancellor, his drinking partner, his political ally — to be the most senior churchman in England. Thomas Becket. Archbishop of Canterbury.

Henry’s calculation is obvious. The Church in England has its own courts, its own law, its own privileges — and they keep getting in the way of his royal reforms. If his own man is in charge of the Church, the conflicts vanish.

He calculated wrong.

[pause]

Becket, the moment he is consecrated, undergoes a religious conversion of a kind that is difficult to fake. He stops being Henry’s friend. He stops being Henry’s chancellor. He becomes — fully, with every fibre — the Church’s champion. He defends the Church’s courts against the king’s. He defends the Church’s privileges. He excommunicates royal officials who cross him.

The friendship dies. The fight that replaces it gets uglier and uglier for eight years. Becket flees to France. The king confiscates Church property. The Pope threatens to interdict England. Negotiations break down. Negotiations resume. Negotiations break down again.

[pause]

In December 1170, a kind of peace is signed. Becket returns to Canterbury. Within a week of his return, he excommunicates the bishops who had crowned the king’s son in his absence — including, by extension, those who had supported them. The court is in uproar.

At a Christmas court in Normandy, Henry — drunk, frustrated, surrounded by sycophants — says, in his great rage, words something like: Will no one rid me of this turbulent priest?

[slow]

Four of his knights take him literally.

They ride to the coast. They cross the Channel. They go to Canterbury.

On the evening of December 29th, 1170, during vespers — evening prayer — they walk into Canterbury Cathedral with their swords drawn. The monks try to bar the doors. Becket — astonishingly — orders them open. He says he will not run from God’s house.

The knights find him in the north transept. They demand he go with them. He refuses. One of them strikes the top of his head with a sword.

Thomas Becket falls to the floor of his own cathedral. The four knights surround him. They go on striking until he is dead. One of them — and this is the detail that turned all of Christendom — uses the tip of his sword to scrape the top of Becket’s skull onto the cathedral floor.

Then they ride away.

[pause, full beat]

[slow]

The shock across Europe is impossible to overstate. An archbishop, murdered in his own cathedral, in front of his own monks, by knights acting on the king’s word. The Pope canonises Becket within three years. Pilgrims start arriving at Canterbury by the thousands.

And Prior Philip of Kingsbridge — now an old man, sixty-something, white-haired — saw, in Becket’s death, the political opportunity he had been waiting his whole life for. Not for himself. For the truth.

Philip leads a march of monks and clergy on Canterbury. He preaches at every village they pass. He writes letters to bishops across England. He makes himself, quietly, the moral voice of the country’s outrage. By the spring, public opinion has shifted so far against the king that Henry — Henry II, the most powerful king in Europe — has no choice.

In 1174, four years after the murder, Henry walks barefoot from the edge of Canterbury to Becket’s tomb. He kneels. The monks of Canterbury take rods. They flog him. Publicly. On bare skin. In front of a watching crowd.

The most powerful king in Europe, beaten with sticks at the tomb of the man his words had murdered.

The Church wins. Wins for a generation.

[pause]

[brisker]

And in the wake of this — with the Church suddenly emboldened, with the king suddenly humbled, with old crimes suddenly being looked at again — Philip moves on William Hamleigh.

He has been quietly gathering evidence for years. Witnesses to the wool fair massacre — those who survived, those whose children did not. Witnesses to the rape at Bartholomew’s castle — Aliena will testify. Witnesses to the burnings, the hangings, the protection rackets William ran across Shiring for two decades. Servants who saw. Priests who took confessions. A whole shadow record of a man who had been protected only by the indifference of a weak king.

William is summoned to a royal court. Not a Church court — a royal court, under Henry’s new common law. He cannot use the privilege of clergy. He cannot pay his way out. The evidence is overwhelming.

William Hamleigh is condemned to death.

He is hanged. On the same gallows in Shiring on which he had hanged so many others. He is fifty-something by now. His mother Regan — already dead from old age — is not there to watch. Aliena is. She stands at the back of the crowd, in a plain dress, and she watches him drop.

[pause]

What she feels at that moment is, the book tells us, not satisfaction. Not really. She is in her forties. The seventeen-year-old who had sworn vengeance is a person she has not been for a very long time. Watching William die is — and this is the brutal honesty of Follett’s writing — almost an anti-climax. She feels something close to boredom. It is a thing that should have happened long ago.

She walks home with her brother through the market square. They do not speak.

[pause]

[slow]

Now — Waleran.

Jack has spent twenty years quietly gathering the proof. The chalice itself — the actual jewelled chalice from 1123 — has been traced, by Jack’s persistent inquiries through ecclesiastical contacts in France, to a treasury where Waleran had quietly deposited it half a lifetime ago. The chalice is recovered. The provenance is documented.

A Church council is convened. Philip presents the case.

He lays out: the embassy of 1123. The missing chalice. The hanging of Jacques de Cherbourg, an innocent man. The three men who corroborated. Two now dead. One — sitting in the council chamber, in his bishop’s robes — still alive.

Waleran denies everything. But the chalice is there on the table. Witnesses are produced — old men, brought from France, who had been at the embassy and who had been told, at the time, not to ask questions. Now they ask. Now they answer.

The council strips Waleran of his bishopric. They strip him of his lands. They send him to a remote monastery to live out his life as a simple monk, in penance, until he dies.

He dies, alone, within two years.

[pause]

The third name on the curse. Gone.

[pause, full beat]

[slow, building]

The cathedral is consecrated in — let’s call it 1174 or so. A spring morning. Sun. The first true Gothic cathedral in England. The west front rises in three tiers of carved stone with pointed arches. The nave is two hundred feet long. The clerestory is fifty feet of stained glass. The light through the windows on the south side falls onto the floor of the nave in moving panels of red and blue and green and gold.

Philip presides. He is seventy years old. This is the last great mass of his life. He has spent forty years on this building. The boy he hired in the ashes of the old cathedral is buried in the cemetery outside; the man who finished it is standing in the front row.

Jack stands in the front row with Aliena. Tommy is twenty-five now, a master mason in his own right, who will inherit the work. There are younger children too — the second and third children of Jack and Aliena, born in the years of peace.

Martha — Tom’s daughter — stands next to them. Never married. Devoted, all her life, to the cathedral and to Jack.

And at the back of the church, under one of the great stained-glass windows of the south transept, in a plain woollen dress, stands a sixty-five-year-old woman with red hair gone white.

[pause]

Ellen.

The girl from the prologue. The girl who had stood at the back of a crowd in a market square in 1123, pregnant, and watched a Frenchman die for a crime he had not committed, and looked at the three men who had hanged him, and had cursed them out loud.

She has come down out of the forest, for the first time in over forty years, to see her son’s cathedral consecrated.

She is the only person in the building who knows, fully, what this moment really means.

[pause]

She looks up at the high vault. She looks at the light coming through the glass. She looks at her son standing in the front row with his arm around his wife. And she thinks — although the book is too disciplined to say so directly — they are gone. All three of them. The knight. The monk. The priest. They are gone, and the man they murdered has built this.

[pause, full beat]

[normal voice, slow]

That’s the book.

A book that begins with three men watching a hanging in a small market town ends with all three of them disgraced or dead, and the son of the man they hanged standing inside a building unlike any his country has ever seen.

A book that begins with a homeless mason in the snow ends with that mason’s stepson finishing the cathedral the mason died trying to build.

A book that begins with a seventeen-year-old girl swearing in an empty hall to get her brother his earldom back ends with her brother, twenty years later, as Earl of Shiring.

A book whose middle is a chronicler’s line that “Christ and his saints slept” ends with Christ and his saints, you might say, awake again — light through the glass, the king flogged at the tomb, a curse landing fifty years after it was sworn.

The Pillars of the Earth are not, in the end, the cathedral. They are the four people who built it. Tom. Philip. Jack. Aliena. Each of them carrying, on their shoulders, the weight that none of them could carry alone.

[pause, full beat]

That’s The Pillars of the Earth.

The curse takes fifty years to land.

But it lands.

Delivery notes

  • The Becket murder — this is your final great setpiece. Slow down for the cathedral, the vespers, the sword. The detail of the sword tip scraping his skull onto the floor is the one piece of brutality that needs to land precisely; deliver it flat, without drama.
  • Henry’s penance — short, factual, weighty. The most powerful king in Europe, flogged. Let that sit.
  • William’s hanging — the surprise here is Aliena feeling boredom. Don’t telegraph it. Deliver it flat. “She feels something close to boredom.” Let your listener react.
  • Waleran’s downfall — this should feel almost anticlimactic. Quiet. A council chamber. Old men giving testimony. The chalice on a table. That’s the entire scale of the closure. Don’t ham it up.
  • Ellen at the back of the cathedral — this is the emotional climax of the whole six-day arc. Drop volume. Slow all the way down. She is the bridge to the prologue. “She is the only person in the building who knows, fully, what this moment really means.” Pause hard before “They are gone.”
  • The final triplet — “The curse takes fifty years to land. But it lands.” This is your last line. Pause before “But it lands.” Same as Day 1. Same shape. End where you started.

After Day 6 — what to say if your friend asks “and then what?”

The book ends here. There are sequels — World Without End (set 200 years later, same town), A Column of Fire (200 years after that), The Evening and the Morning (a prequel set 100 years before). Each is its own story; the characters do not survive into them, only the cathedral and the town.

If your friend wants to read the book itself after this — and many do — tell them honestly: it is long, it is occasionally a bit repetitive, and the prose is workmanlike rather than beautiful. But the engineering of the plot is something you can learn from. Ken Follett spent three years writing this book and twenty thinking about it before that. Almost nothing in it is wasted.
